2.3 International Bargaining and Militarized Force

Governments’ trade policies not only shift the domestic distribution of income, they also impose externalities, causing changes in the foreign distribution of income. Consider a tariff increase in the foreign country. As a result of higher domestic prices, imports contract and domestic producers pick up the slack. Consumers face higher prices, cutting into their welfare. The foreign government trades increases in producer surplus and tariff revenue for decreases in consumer surplus. This domestic political economy of trade is well-theorized (Rogowski 1989; Grossman and Helpman 1994; Hiscox 2002). This behavior has implications for the home country as well, however. A tariff increase causes a downward shift in the international price. This causes a reduction in the profits of producers in the home country and an increase in consumption. The foreign government’s actions not only harms its own consumers, they also harm producers in the home country. When the home government weighs the interests of these producers above those of the consumers, the foreign country’s behavior imposes a negative externality.

Governments disatisfied with the market access conditions abroad have numerous tools at their disposal to seek changes in the trade policies of other governments. The most direct strategy is to simply solve trade conflicts through trade negotiations. Governments can trade policy concessions in order to realize pareto efficient outcomes (Grossman and Helpman 1995; Davis 2004; Maggi 2016). Of course, such negotiations have distributional consequences that may favor certain actors at the expense of others. If trade policy instruments are the only tools available to secure bargaining advantage, then governments with weaker preference intensity will be forced to adopt trade policies closer to the ideal points of their trade partners. When trade policy conflicts of interest are sufficiently intense, these governments might prefer to tie non-trade inducements (positive or negative) to trade negotiations. The further the range of trade-only bargains is from a given government’s ideal point, the greater its incentives to manipulate others’ incentives through coercion. In the anarchy of world politics, coercion can always - in principle - take the form of threats, displays, or uses of military force (Fearon 1997).16 Instances of ‘gunboat diplomacy’ - in which military threats were unambiguously tied to market access conditions - are not difficult to find in the historical record. The question considered here is under what circumstances governments will find pure trade negotiations insufficient to their ends and will be willing to risk military conflict to pursue these ends.

Here, I assume that war can be employed to replace a government with a type of the victor’s choosing. In the context of the model presented in the previous subsection, this means choosing the \(a_i\) parameter that conditions the foreign government’s choice of trade policy. Since \(a_i\) determines the ideal bilateral trade policy of each government, war can be used to move the ideal point of a trading partner closer to one’s own. This in turn moves the reversion point of the trade policy bargaining game and the set of feasible bargaining outcomes.17 This also imposes limits on the magnitude of concessions that can be extracted through war. If the home government had control of both trade policy instruments \(\left\{ \tau_i, \tau_j \right\}\), it could use this power to redistribute an arbitrary amount of utility from the foreign government to itself. Because victorious governments only get to choose the (bounded) type of their negotiating partner, they can only obtain the concession that the most-favorable type of partner government would be willing to grant. When contemplating a war, therefore, governments consider both the costs of the war and the benefits they would accrue if they were able to bargain with a partner holding more ‘dovish’ trade policy preferences. They also consider the outcome in which they lose a war and are replaced by a government of the foreign country’s choosing. In this case, the trade policy negotiated by the foreign-installed government is implemented, but the welfare changes resulting from the policy change are weighted by the old government’s utility function.18 This assumption is consistent with the conceptualization of the government as an aggregation of substate actors. When contemplating a war, this aggregation considers its (weighted) change in welfare as a result of the imposition of new trade policies under the foreign-installed government. Importantly, unlike most bargaining and war games, this aggregation does not cease to exist (\(G_i \neq 0\)) in the case of a war defeat.

The interaction between the governments proceeds as follows. I consider a take it or leave it bargaining game in which governments can choose whether to settle their differences through pure ‘trade talks’ (Grossman and Helpman 1995) or through the use of military force. Governments are each endowed with some exogenously-given military capability \(\omega_i \in \mathbb{R}_+\).19 A possible extension to the model would endogenize military capability and seek to explain states’ military investment decisions as a function of political/economic primitives. This might be a more tractable dependent variable than conflict. Feedback welcome here. A contest function \(\rho: \left\{ \omega_i, \omega_j \right\} \rightarrow [0, 1]\) determines the probability that government \(i\) prevails in war for control of the type of \(j\)’s government. This contest function is assumed to be increasing and weakly concave in \(\omega_i\), to be decreasing and weakly convex in \(\omega_j\) and to satisfy the law of total probability \(\rho(\omega_i, \omega_j) + \rho(\omega_j, \omega_i) = 1\). The commonly used ‘additive’ contest function (Jia, Skaperdas, and Vaidya 2013) satisfies these conditions for ω ∈ ℝ++ with
\[ \rho(\omega_i, \omega_j) = \frac{\omega_i}{\omega_i + \omega_j} \]
 If either state chooses to engage in a war, both governments pay fixed proportion of their post-war welfare, denoted \(c_i \in [0,1]\). Country \(i\) wins the war with probability \(\rho(\omega_i, \omega_j)\) and can then replace \(j\)’s government with an alternative government of its choosing. The value of winning a war stems from the ability to choose the type of government that one will bargain with in future trade policy negotiations. Of course, if \(i\) loses the war, then \(j\) replaces \(i\)’s government.

In the bargaining game, government \(j\) offers a trade policy pair \(\left\{ \tau_i^j, \tau_j^j \right\}\) and some side payment \(R\) to country \(i\).20 Grossman and Helpman (1995) prove the distributional equivalence between 1) a fixed trade policy pair \(\left\{ \tau_i^j, \tau_j^j \right\}\) and a side payment and 2) a fixed trade policy ratio (\(\tau_i / \tau_j\)) and a trade policy ‘level’ \(\tau_j\). Essentially, they show that governments can use trade policy to implement an arbitrary division of utility through its affect on tariff revenues (or subsidy costs). I introduce a side payment here to distinguish between efficiency and distributional considerations, but the analysis holds if governments are prohibited from using side payments. Unlike in Grossman and Helpman (1995), the side payment is assumed to contribute directly to governments’ utilities, where \[G_i^t = G_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) + R\] Then, country \(i\) takes one of three actions.

In the bargaining game described in Lemma 2, the governments choose the pareto efficient trade policy, and then redistribute income amongst themselves such that \(j\) is indifferent between the realized bargain and the trade wars outcome. The resulting utility for government \(i\), therefore, is dependent on both its own type \(a_i\), but also the type of its negotiating partner, \(a_j\). Taking this bargain as given, what type of government would country \(i\) most like to bargain with? Since government \(i\) will receive \(\overline{G}_i(\tau_i^t, \tau_j^t; a_i, a_j)\) in any bargain, it would like to choose \(a_j\) to maximize this quantity. The utility the victorious government receives, \(\overline{G}_i\) is simply its utility under the bargaining game in which it optimally chooses the type of its trading partner. The utility of the losing government is slightly more complicated. \(\underline{G}_j\) is determined by how the old government evaluates the policy agreed upon by the victor and the new puppet government. It is therefore a function of the original type of country \(j\)’s government, \(a_j\), the type of the victorious government, \(a_i\), and the type of the puppet installed by the victor, \(a_j^{i \star}\).

For ease of exposition, consider the case in which the foreign country, \(j\), has proposal power, both before and after hot wars. This makes it relatively straightforward to determine under what conditions country \(i\) will prefer to initiate a hot war, and when this option will bind in determining bargaining outcomes. In the case of a hot war, the victor will replace the loser’s government with its ideal, and \(j\) will push \(i\) to its reversion point, making it indifferent between the trade wars outcome and its offer. Following Lemma 2, this will yield the following utility for \(i\): \[ \left[ \rho_i(\omega_i, \omega_j) G_i^w(a_i, a_j^{i \star}) + (1 - \rho_i(\omega_i, \omega_j) G_i^w(a_i^{j \star}, a_j) \right] ( 1 - c_i ) \] If the absence of the hot wars option, government \(i\) simply receives its trade wars utility, \(G_i^w(a_i, a_j)\).

Government \(i\) will reject any offer and declare war whenever such an offer leaves it worse off than it expects to be under hot wars. Therefore, ‘hot wars’ will bind in determining bargaining outcomes whenever \[ \left[ \rho_i(\omega_i, \omega_j) G_i^w(a_i, a_j^{i \star}) + (1 - \rho_i(\omega_i, \omega_j) G_i^w(a_i^{j \star}, a_j) \right] ( 1 - c_i ) > G_i^w(a_i, a_j) \]
