2.2 Political-Economic Equilibrium (Trade Wars)

The previous subsection characterizes how the international economy will respond to an arbitrary set of trade policies. Now I describe the domestic political process through which those policies are determined. Note that the international price of Good 1 is determined by the trade policy choices of the governments, allowing us to write \(\pi(\tau_i, \tau_j)\). Since \(p_i = \tau_i \pi\), domestic prices are also a function of the trade policies, giving \(p_i(\tau_i, \tau_j)\).

In the next subsection, I will allow governments to bargain over the bilateral trade policy, \(\left\{ \tau_i, \tau_j \right\}\). For now, I assume that the governments are engaged in a simple, noncooperative price-setting game that I will refer to as ‘trade wars,’ following Grossman and Helpman (1995).10 In the bargaining game presented in the next subsection, this will serve as one of the reversion points available to the governments.

The game proceeds as follows:

  1. Producers present contribution schedules to the government, specifying the amount they will contribute for each potential pair of realized home-foreign trade policies, \(\left\{ \tau_i, \tau_j \right\}\).
  2. Governments each choose trade policies.
  3. Policies are implemented. Contributions accrue to the government and production and consumption occurs.

This subsection characterizes the trade policies and equilibrium contributions that emerge in the Nash Equilibrium of this game. I first seek to characterize the menu of contributions that will be offered to the governments by producers. Producers seek to maximize their net welfare. Letting \(C_i(\tau_i, \tau_j)\), denote the political contributions of producers in country \(i\), net producer welfare can be written:11 Since \(p_i\) is a function of the trade policies, we can write \(\Pi(p_i)\) as \(\Pi(\tau_i, \tau_j)\). \[ W_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) = \Pi_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) - C_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) \]

As discussed in the introduction, governments seek to maximize a weighted average of social welfare and political contributions. Governments are characterized by an exogenous parameter \(a_i \in [0,1]\) which represents the weight they place on social welfare relative to campaign contributions. Kleptocrats \((a_i \rightarrow 0)\) care only about the political contributions and the tariff revenue (or subsidy costs) induced by their policy choices. As \(a_i\) approaches 1, the government becomes a pure social welfare maximizer, placing no weight on contributions. Government welfare is given by:12 Note that this government welfare function differs slightly from that of Grossman and Helpman (1994), where \(G = C + a ( \Pi + r + S )\) and \(a \in \mathbb{R}_+\). Here, \(a\) is bounded in the unit interval and all governments weight tariff revenue equally. The assumption that tariff revenue is unweighted for all governments ensures that best response curves are smooth. In the Grossman-Helpman formulation, unrepresentative governments (\(a \rightarrow 0\)) do not internalize the cost of policy interventions (\(r(\tau_i, \tau_j)\)) which can yield extremely distortionary optimal policy and sometimes precludes the existence of an equilibrium. \[ G_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) = (1 - a) C_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) + a \left[\Pi_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) + S_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) \right] + r(\tau_i, \tau_j) \]

Lemma 1 shows that in an equilibrium of this domestic game, contributions induce the government to maximize a weighted sum of producer welfare and social welfare.13 Lemma 1 is a special case of Proposition 1 in Grossman and Helpman (1994), which itself was an application of Bernheim and Whinston (1986). Equilibrium contributions are sufficient to make the government indifferent between implementing equilibrium policy and its optimal policy when \(C_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) = 0\).

Lemma 1: For an arbitrary foreign (home) trade policy, \(\tau_j\), let \(\tau_i^0\) denote the government’s optimal policy in the absence of political contributions (\(C_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) = 0\)) and \(g_i(\tau_i; \tau_j)\) its associated welfare. \[ \tau_i^0 \in \underset{\tau_i}{\operatorname{argmax}} g_i(\tau_i; \tau_j) \] In a domestic political economic equilibrium, the government chooses trade policy to maximize a weighted sum of producer and social welfare \[ \tau_i^\star \in \underset{\tau_i}{\operatorname{argmax}} \Pi_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) + a S_i(\tau_i, \tau_j) + r(\tau_i, \tau_j) \] and equilibrium political contributions from producers of Good 1 (\(C_i^\star\)) satisfy \[ C_i^\star(\tau_i^0, \tau_i^\star; \tau_j) = \Pi_i(\tau_i^\star, \tau_j) + a S_i(\tau_i^\star, \tau_j) + r(\tau_i^\star, \tau_j) - g_i(\tau_i^0; \tau_j) \] Proof: See Appendix.

Notice that \(g_i\) and \(G_i\) converge to one another as \(a \rightarrow 1\). As a result, \(\tau^0 \rightarrow \tau^\star\). Of course, this comes at the expense of producers abroad. This is the conflict of interest that is explored in the next subsection.

Definition 2 (Trade Wars): In a noncooperative Nash Equilibrium, government trade policies maximize their contribution-induced utility and constitute best responses to one another \[ \tau_i^\star \in \underset{\tau_i}{\operatorname{argmax}} \Pi_i(\tau_i, \tau_j^\star) + a S_i(\tau_i, \tau_j^\star) + r(\tau_i, \tau_j^\star) \] \[ \tau_j^\star \in \underset{\tau_j}{\operatorname{argmax}} \Pi_j(\tau_j, \tau_i^\star) + a S_j(\tau_j, \tau_i^\star) + r(\tau_j, \tau_i^\star) \] Political contributions, \(C_i^\star(\tau_i^0, \tau_i^\star, ;\tau_j^\star)\), satisfy Lemma 1.

This equilibrium is taken as the reversion point in the bargaining model of Grossman and Helpman (1995). In the next subsection, I consider an alternative bargaining game in which governments can employ military force in pursuit of their policy objectives.
